AI for Product Marketing

Introduction

Um, hey everyone. Welcome. Uh, thanks for joining me today. My name is Jordan, and I'm a product marketer at a fast-moving tech company. And today I want to talk about something that has genuinely changed the way I work over the past year. Using AI to become a more effective tech product marketer.

I use it every single day now, and I honestly don't know how I'd keep up without it. Let's get into it.

1. Competitive Research

The first area where AI has made a massive difference is competitive research. As a product marketer, you're expected to know your competitive landscape cold. And, uh, that used to take hours every week. Reading blog posts, scanning press releases, digging through G2 reviews.

Now I paste in a competitor's website copy or their latest press release, ask AI to summarize their positioning, flag their weaknesses, and identify target personas. What used to take three or four hours I can now do in under an hour.

2. Customer & Persona Research

The second area is customer and persona research. Um, one of the hardest things in product marketing is deeply understanding your buyer. What language do they use? What keeps them up at night? What does success look like in their role?

I take transcripts from customer interviews, sales calls, and support tickets, and I feed them to AI to extract recurring themes and pull out exact phrases customers use. That last part is huge for copywriting. When your messaging uses your customer's own words, everything lands better.

I've also started using AI to stress-test personas. I describe a persona and ask the AI what objections they'd raise, what would make them skeptical, what they'd need before taking a meeting. It's like having a sparring partner who's read every B2B buyer study ever written.

3. Messaging & Positioning

Okay, third area: messaging and positioning. AI is a fantastic thought partner here, but the key is bringing real context. I don't just say write me a tagline. That produces generic garbage. Instead, I give it the product, the persona, the competition, the key differentiators, and ask for five or six positioning angles targeting different emotional drivers.

Reacting to a set of options is faster than creating from scratch, and I end up with sharper positioning in a fraction of the time. The AI acts like a well-read editor throughout the whole messaging process.

4. Launch Planning & GTM

Fourth area: launch planning and go-to-market strategy. I use AI to pressure-test GTM plans. Right channels, sequencing, key risks, success metrics. Um, it doesn't replace strategic thinking, but it surfaces blind spots and asks clarifying questions in ways that push me to think more rigorously.

Launch calendars, stakeholder briefing docs, creative briefs. AI takes a lot of that scaffolding off my plate.

5. Content Creation & Enablement

Fifth area: content creation and sales enablement. Blog posts, battlecards, email sequences, LinkedIn content. AI helps with all of it. But the best results come when I bring real substance and use AI to shape it, not when I ask it to write from scratch.

My workflow is: I do the thinking first. I jot down the key points, the stories, the data. Then I use AI to structure it, smooth the prose, and repurpose it for different formats. A long-form post becomes a LinkedIn thread becomes a sales email. That repurposing is where AI saves me the most time.

Ah, and sales enablement specifically. Writing battlecards is tedious. Feed AI your product strengths, competitor weaknesses, and win-loss patterns, and you've got a solid first draft in minutes.

6. Analytics & Performance

Sixth, and final area: analytics and performance. I'm not a data analyst. I can pull numbers, but I don't always know what story they're telling. I paste campaign performance data into AI and ask: what patterns do you see here? What explains this drop in conversion? What would you test to improve it? It consistently surfaces hypotheses I wouldn't have found on my own.

Wrap-Up

Okay, let me bring it together. Um, AI isn't replacing product marketers. What it's doing is eliminating the slow, grind-y parts of the job so you can spend more time on things that actually require judgment. Building relationships with customers, making strategic bets, crafting a point of view that's genuinely differentiated.

The marketers I see struggling are spending forty hours on things that could take fifteen. The ones thriving have learned to collaborate with these tools. So if you're not already experimenting, start small. Pick one workflow. Try it. You might be surprised.

Alright, that's it from me. Thanks for watching, and, uh, I'll see you in the next one.
